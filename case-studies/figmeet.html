overview

design teams are some of the most collaborative people in any product org. yet every review session pulls them out of figma - into zoom, slack, loom, and back. by the time everyone's on the same screen, the context is already gone.

figmeet is a figma plugin that brings voice, video, and live presence directly into the canvas. the call lives where the design lives. no tab switching, no re-explaining, no lost thread.

the problem

by the time the screen was shared on zoom, everyone had already forgotten what they were looking at in figma.

4+

tools the average design team switches between in a single review - figma, zoom, slack, loom.

~30 min

lost per session to context switching, screen share delays, and re-explaining the same decision.

68%

of designers say verbal feedback rarely matches the comment left in figma. the loop breaks every time.

the core insight

the problem isn't that teams don't talk enough. it's that the conversation and the design have never been in the same place.

the solution

figmeet extends figma's native multiplayer with voice, video, and anchored reactions. each feature was designed to remove one specific reason designers reach for another tab.

design process

four stages. each one narrowed the problem down until only one solution was left.

01

research

interviewed 8 designers and 4 developers across remote-first teams. mapped their exact toolchain during design reviews. the pattern was unmistakable - figma, then zoom, then figma again.

02

define

the plugin must feel invisible. it cannot compete with figma's UI for attention. every interaction had to feel like it already belonged there.

03

explore

early concepts took over the left panel entirely. too aggressive. iterated to a collapsible right tray - present when needed, a strip of avatars when not.

04

test & refine

ran 3 moderated prototype sessions. cursor spotlight validated immediately. a proposed hand-raise button was cut - nobody used it. what teams needed was simpler than expected.

what i learned
designing inside someone else's product

figma’s design language isn’t just a visual style - people use it every day and instantly notice when something feels out of place. the challenge with figmeet was making every interaction feel like it already belonged inside figma, instead of feeling like a separate layer added on top.

that constraint forced a lot more intentional decision-making. several features were removed, not because they were bad ideas, but because they didn’t improve the core experience enough to justify the space.

the biggest lesson from the project was understanding that collaboration tools should stay out of the way. if users focus on the discussion and the work itself, the product is doing what it’s supposed to do.
