access healthcare

a new visual identity for a $1B+ RCM company that looked like a company from 2014.

before after
overview

access healthcare is a $1B+ revenue cycle management company - they handle medical billing, coding, and accounts receivable for hospitals and health systems across the US. 140,000+ employees. fortune 500 clients.

the website didn't say any of that. it said: generic healthcare company, circa 2014. the visual language failed the business it was supposed to represent - and it failed it at the exact moment decision-makers were evaluating vendors.

the problem

a CFO deciding between RCM partners lands on your website. in 8 seconds, you either feel like a category leader - or a vendor they'll skip.

8 sec

average time a B2B buyer spends on a landing page before deciding to stay or leave.

~15

direct RCM competitors with more modern digital presence - Optum, R1, Ensemble, nThrive.

0

clear hierarchy on the current homepage. everything shouts, so nothing lands.

who buys this
MK
Michael K., VP Revenue Cycle
Health system, 12 hospitals · actively evaluating RCM vendors
has 20 minutes between meetings to make a first cut.
what he does on the site
  • skims the hero for a headline that matches his problem
  • scrolls to case studies – wants to see his specialty
  • checks "who we help" – does this company get hospitals?
  • looks for the phone number or demo CTA to forward to his team
what he needs to feel
  • this company has done this before, at scale
  • they know what revenue cycle actually means operationally
  • i can trust them with my billing infrastructure
  • they're worth putting in front of my CFO
the design brief

stop looking like every other RCM company. trust is the product – and trust is communicated before a single word is read.

the redesign

one landing page. one clear message. the redesign applies a bold typographic hierarchy, a differentiated colour system, and a section structure designed around how Michael actually moves through the page.

designing for B2B buyers

in consumer design, you design for delight. in B2B, you design for confidence. every visual choice is either adding to a buyer's certainty that this company can handle their complexity – or quietly subtracting from it. that constraint sharpened every decision on this project.
